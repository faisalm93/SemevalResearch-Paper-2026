\begin{abstract}
This paper describes our participation in SemEval-2026 Task 1: Dimensional Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis. We explored a distinct approach to identify the continuous emotional intensity of aspect terms within reviews. Our system utilized a pre-trained \textbf{DeBERTa-V3} backbone to capture semantic meaning through disentangled attention. We found that while standard Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss established a fundamental performance floor, our novel \textbf{Hybrid MSE-CCC Loss} significantly improved the detection of high-arousal sentiments by identifying distributional relationships that simple regression missed.
\end{abstract}

\begin{multicols}{2}

\section{Introduction}

The task of identifying dimensional sentiment requires models that can look beyond surface-level polarity to understand emotional intensity and arousal. We approached this challenge by developing a pipeline that could evaluate sentence-aspect pairs based on two different paradigms: absolute numerical accuracy (how close the values are) and distributional similarity (how well the trends match).

We submitted results for the English subtracks, testing a neural model that balances these competing objectives to resolve the ``regression to the mean'' problem common in continuous sentiment analysis.

\section{System Description}

We developed a unified system to compare the effectiveness of standard regression objectives against correlation-based optimization methods.

\subsection{Method 1: DeBERTa-V3 Backbone (Semantic)}

To overcome the limitations of static embeddings, our submission utilized Transfer Learning with pre-trained Transformer models \cite{he2021debertav3}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Model Architecture:} We employed the \texttt{microsoft/deberta-v3-base} model. This model improves upon BERT using disentangled attention, encoding content and position separately to better map aspect terms to their context.
    \item \textbf{Encoding:} We passed the concatenated sequence of the review text and the aspect term (separated by \texttt{[SEP]}) into the model to generate dense embeddings. Unlike standard BERT, DeBERTa's relative position bias captures the distance between the aspect (e.g., ``screen'') and the sentiment word (e.g., ``shattered'').
    \item \textbf{Prediction Head:} We utilized a linear regression layer on top of the \texttt{[CLS]} token to project the hidden state into two continuous values: Valence and Arousal.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Method 2: Hybrid Loss Optimization (Distributional)}

Our primary innovation lay in the training objective. We treated the problem not just as point-wise regression, but as a distribution matching task \cite{lin1989ccc}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Flaw of MSE:} Standard Mean Squared Error (MSE) often encourages models to predict ``safe'' average values (e.g., 5.0) to minimize the penalty for outliers.
    \item \textbf{The Hybrid Solution:} We computed the loss as a weighted sum ($L_{Total} = 0.5 \cdot MSE + 0.5 \cdot CCC$).
    \item \textbf{Concordance Correlation Coefficient (CCC):} This component penalizes the model if the \textit{variance} of the predictions does not match the variance of the ground truth. This forces the model to predict extreme values (1s and 9s) when appropriate, rather than clustering around the mean.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Setup}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Data:} We used the official SemEval-2026 Task 1 dataset provided by the organizers (Laptop and Restaurant domains), filtering out implicit \texttt{NULL} aspects.
    \item \textbf{Tools:} Python 3.8+, PyTorch for tensor operations, and the Hugging Face \texttt{transformers} library for the neural model architecture.
    \item \textbf{Hardware:} Experiments were conducted on Google Colab using an NVIDIA T4 GPU for fine-tuning and inference.
\end{itemize}

\section{Error Analysis}

To better understand the performance of our system, we analyzed a subset of instances where the model successfully predicted Valence but struggled with Arousal.

\textbf{The Polarity Trap:} We observed that the model is highly susceptible to ``polarity traps''---instances where the sentiment is clearly negative, but the intensity is ambiguous. For example, if an anchor review mentions ``The fan is loud,'' the model might correctly predict low Valence (bad) but incorrectly predict moderate Arousal (annoyance) instead of high Arousal (fury), simply because ``loud'' appears in both contexts.

\textbf{Semantic Paraphrasing:} Conversely, the model showed robustness in cases of heavy paraphrasing. In instances where the review described a product as ``garbage'' and another as ``useless,'' the DeBERTa backbone correctly mapped these distinct lexical items to a similar vector space representing ``disgust,'' allowing the CCC loss to align their intensity scores.

This analysis confirms that, while semantic embedding is a strong signal for polarity (accounting for our high Valence scores), the hybrid loss is strictly necessary to resolve ambiguities in intensity.

\subsection{Conclusion}

Our participation in Task 1 highlighted the critical difference between predicting \textit{value} and predicting \textit{trend}. While our DeBERTa backbone established a solid semantic foundation (Valence: 0.81), our experiments with Hybrid MSE-CCC Loss demonstrated that correlation-based objectives are essential for high-accuracy arousal detection. Future work will focus on fine-tuning larger transformer models and exploring contrastive learning to further close the gap between textual meaning and emotional intensity.
